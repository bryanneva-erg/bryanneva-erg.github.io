Atmospheric Lifetimes

  • Atmospheric Lifetimes

    This interactive graphic desribes the process that affect how long gases stay in the atmosphere and the ways in which they can leave.

  • Emissions

    Emissions are how gases enter the atmosphere. They include natural sources, but many warming gases can only be created by humans.

  • Long-lived gases

    Long-lived gases can leave the atmosphere, but tend to do so in slower processes. They tend to be more chemically stable and exercise their warming effect over hundred or thousands of years.

  • Short-lived gases

    Short-lived gases are defiend by having highly efficient pathways for leaving the atmosphere. Even though they do not stay in the atmosphere beyond a couple decades, their warming effects can be among the highest of all.

  • Loss Pathways

    Once emitted, both short-lived and long-lived gases can leave the atmosphere through several different mechanisms

  • Chemical deposition

    Many gases react with sunlight or other trace molecules in the atmosphere. Once they react, they transform into other molecules that might no longer warm the Earth. In other cases, they may become even stronger.

  • Wet/dry deposition

    When gases get absorbed into rain, snow, or fog, they can fall to the ground as precipitation. They can also combine with dust or smoke to fall to the ground as solids. Once no longer airborne, the gases are on Earth's surface.

  • Absorption by surface

    From bacteria to plants, many organisms have a biological need to convert certain greenhouse gases, included CH4 and CO2. Gases can also get absorbed into the oceans, which represent a major sink for long-lived gases.
