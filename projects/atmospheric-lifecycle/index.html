Atmospheric Lifetimes

  • Atmospheric Lifetimes

    This interactive graphic describes the processes that affect how long gases stay in the atmosphere and the ways in which they can leave.

  • Emissions

    Gases enter the atmosphere when they are emitted from natural sources (resulting from biological processes) or from human activities. Many warming gases can only be created by human activities.

  • Long-lived gases

    Long-lived gases leave the atmosphere slowly. They tend to be more chemically stable, reacting less readily with sunlight or other gases in the atmosphere. As a result, their warming effect continues over hundreds or thousands of years.

  • Short-lived gases

    Short-lived gases are more reactive than long-lived gases and thus leave the atmosphere at a faster rate. Though they only stay in the atmosphere for days to decades, many of these gases can trap more heat in a short time than others do over many years.

  • Loss Pathways

    Once emitted, both short-lived and long-lived gases can leave the atmosphere in several different ways.

  • Chemical deposition

    Many gases react with sunlight or other trace molecules in the atmosphere. Some transform into other molecules that no longer trap heat. Others transform into substances that continue to trap heat, sometimes even more heat than the original gas.

  • Wet/dry deposition

    When gases get absorbed into rain, snow, or fog, they can fall to the ground as precipitation (wet deposition). They can also combine with dust or smoke to fall to the ground as solids (dry deposition).

  • Absorption by surface

    Plants remove CO2 from the atmosphere to use as energy for photosynthesis, which allows carbon to be stored in plants and soils. Oceans also absorb gases and serve as large storage reservoirs for long-lived gases.
